From the Travel Lab Team · April 5, 2026

We spoke to 7 travellers. They all said the same thing.

Travel preparation is broken. Not because the information doesn't exist, but because nobody has made it usable. Here is what we found, and what we're doing about it.

The Research

Seven conversations. One consistent story.

Over the past few weeks, we sat down with seven travellers. Students, working professionals, a PhD candidate, passport holders from across the world, people who travel twice a year and people who travel more than ten times. We weren't trying to sell them anything. We were trying to understand how they actually experience getting ready for a trip.

We asked about their preparation workflows. We asked what stressed them out. We asked what had caught them off guard. We asked where they went for information, and whether they trusted what they found.

7/7 Used multiple disconnected sources
6/7 Relied on friends & peer networks for key info
4/7 Had been concretely caught out

Not one person we spoke to uses a single trusted source for travel preparation. Every single one cobbles together a patchwork of government websites, Google searches, WhatsApp groups, travel forums, and advice from friends who've been before. It's time-consuming, it's inconsistent, and as several of them found out the hard way, it can let you down.

The Core Problem

The information exists. It just isn't usable.

This was the sharpest finding across our conversations. Most of the people we spoke to could, eventually, find what they were looking for. The issue was everything around it: the time spent, the contradictions, the doubt that lingers even when you think you've covered everything. A Dutch leisure traveller who'd recently been to Japan put it simply:

"I end up double-checking everything across multiple sources... you don't know what to trust."

This wasn't an isolated comment. We heard versions of it across almost every conversation. The problem isn't a shortage of information. It's the work of verifying it, reconciling contradictions, and ultimately never feeling entirely certain you've got it right. Four people, four different trips, the same frustration:

"One site said no visa needed, another said there are exceptions... you don't know what to trust." - Preparing for Vietnam
"The one person says they felt very safe there, and the other says they didn't feel safe, and then you get doubt." - On assessing safety online
"The Philippine Embassy in London has a diabolical website, set up to make sure you can't get information rather than can." - On official embassy sources
"I usually just Google because it is fast and flexible. But then again, you don't know what to trust." - On the default approach
Core finding: Travellers don't need more information. They need clear, trustworthy information they can actually act on, before they get to the appointment, the border, or the embarrassing cultural misstep.

When It Goes Wrong

Four in seven had been caught out. Concretely.

There's a difference between finding travel preparation stressful and actually suffering a consequence because of it. Four of our seven interviewees had a specific story. Something that had gone wrong, or very nearly gone wrong, because of something they simply hadn't known. One was a Dutch traveller who'd gone to work at a hotel in Kuala Lumpur:

"I was actually working in Kuala Lumpur, and I needed one more vaccination, and I was not prepared for that. I could not work there without that specific vaccination."

Neither the government website nor any other source she'd consulted had made it clear that the typhoid requirement applied specifically to food industry workers. The information existed somewhere. It just hadn't reached her.

Another interviewee travels regularly with his Filipino wife, and described what happened when he looked into the insurance implications of visiting a flagged area:

"Although it seems like a small detail, if you were in that country and you did need your travel insurance, you'd suddenly find they wouldn't pay out."

The safety advisory was findable. Its consequences were not. And then there's a Jordanian student living in Madrid who travels roughly twelve times a year. His brother flew to Thailand fully expecting to need a visa, and didn't. He spent months navigating Spain's student permit system, at one point needing a lawyer to help him manage expired documents and missed appointments. 'The information is available online,' he told us, 'but it is spread out.'

Passport Matters

Not all passports are created equal. And most platforms ignore this entirely.

One of the starkest patterns across our conversations was how differently the preparation burden falls depending on your passport. Travellers with strong Western passports described visa information as a minor inconvenience, generally easy to find and rarely a real source of stress. Travellers with weaker passports described something categorically different.

One interviewee holds a Jordanian passport, ranked 82nd globally. For him, visa requirements are a big deal for virtually every destination he considers. He relies on a Travel Office in Amman that handles visa appointments on his behalf, a workaround that most travellers would never need to think about. His preparation for a trip to New York began a full year before departure. Meanwhile, a Polish professional in his late 50s who travels more than ten times a year told us:

"With a Polish passport, you can go so many places without a visa or with a visa on arrival, so I don't really worry. I just check."

The gap between those two experiences is enormous, and almost no travel platform currently accounts for it. They describe entry requirements. They don't say for whom. The British traveller with the Filipino wife found this out the hard way when he spent an hour and a half completing a Schengen visa application online, only for the website to crash and lose everything. They didn't take the trip. 'What it should have said,' he told us, 'is: no, actually you've rather left it a bit late, and you shouldn't be trying this unless you absolutely have to.'

What We Heard About Risk

A colour code is not a risk assessment.

Safety information was one of the areas where the gap between what travellers need and what they currently get is widest. Most of our interviewees said they check government advisory websites when assessing a destination. But several described finding the information frustrating to interpret rather than genuinely useful. A Dutch PhD candidate who'd recently visited Kazakhstan summed it up well:

"One country's yellow is not the other country's yellow, so you can determine for yourself whether you think it's an actual risk or not."

She found the codes. She just couldn't translate them into a decision. An amber rating from one government might mean something very different to an amber rating from another, and the websites offer no explanation of the reasoning behind their classifications. She also noted, unprompted, that vaccination websites use language that is technically accurate but practically unhelpful. 'In the end it was just one vaccine you had to take, so it can be a little scary to look at this.' The gap between the frightening-looking website and the simple reality of one jab is a gap that no current tool bridges.

What Travellers Actually Need

Four things. One platform.

When we listened carefully, not just to what people said but to what they were actually trying to do, four distinct needs kept coming up across every conversation.

Functional Consolidate everything in one verified place "I think the key thing is that it is useful if it saves time."
Emotional Feel certain, not just informed "The worries are not about the preparation, they're about: does it all go to plan?"
Social Navigate destinations without cultural embarrassment "Those are sort of cultural things that are nice to know in advance but maybe not as clearly put on official websites."
Epistemic Make sense of risk, not just read a label "One country's yellow is not the other country's yellow."

And then there's a fifth need, surfaced by a Polish professional who travels more than ten times a year. He's been caught out more than once by attractions that required advance booking he simply hadn't known about:

"If you go spontaneously now, you simply won't be able to get into most famous attractions if you haven't booked them with enough notice. That is very 'un-cool' for me."

From a JR Pass in Japan that can only be purchased before you arrive, to Machu Picchu timed entry, to a car park in the Polish mountains that now requires advance booking, the world of travel is filling up with requirements that aren't covered by any standard preparation tool. You have to know to ask. Most people don't find out until they're standing at the gate.

What We're Building

One place for everything you need before you go.

TravelLab is being built to address exactly this. A single platform that brings together visa requirements, vaccination and health guidance, safety advisories with actual context, cultural norms, and practical essentials. Filtered by your passport, tailored to your trip, written in plain language you can actually use.

Not another government portal. Not another travel forum. Something built around the way real people actually plan real trips, starting from the anxiety of not knowing what you don't know, and ending with the confidence that you've genuinely got it covered.
