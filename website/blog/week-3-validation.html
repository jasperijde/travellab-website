From the Travel Lab Team · April 12, 2026 · 5 min read

53 responses. 5 hypotheses. Here's what survived.

We presented our problem validation to our Entrepreneurial Lab cohort on Friday. Two hypotheses confirmed. Two partial. One inconclusive. And a clear path forward.

Last Friday we stood in front of our Entrepreneurial Lab cohort at RSM Erasmus University and walked them through everything we've done over the past few weeks. The interviews. The hypotheses. The survey data from 53 respondents. And what it all means for what we build next.

This blog is a look back at that presentation: what we showed, what the data told us, and the feedback that is now shaping our next move.

The Presentation

From theory to evidence.

Our presentation followed the logic of how we actually worked. We started with our initial theory of the problem: that international travellers, especially those holding weaker passports, face a fragmented, anxiety-inducing process when preparing for trips abroad. Visa rules, vaccination requirements, safety advisories, and cultural norms are scattered across dozens of sources, and none of them feel trustworthy enough on their own.

From there we showed our Story Tree, originally four pillars of value (visa and entry requirements, vaccination and health guidance, safety advisories, and cultural readiness), and the Lean Canvas we used to map our assumptions. We introduced our two customer personas, Joeffrey the spontaneous graduate and Joeffrina the structured professional, and then walked the room through the seven exploratory interviews we conducted.

Those interviews changed things. They revealed that the problem wasn't just information overload. It was fragmentation combined with passport-based inequity. They showed us that cultural readiness, which we'd originally treated as a core pillar, ranked lower than expected as a stated need. And they surfaced something we hadn't anticipated at all: advance booking requirements as a genuine preparation pain point.

So we entered the survey phase with a refined theory: five pillars instead of four, a sharper target segment, and five falsifiable hypotheses to test.

The Results

What 53 travellers told us.

The survey had 25 questions across seven blocks, designed to test each hypothesis with clear thresholds for confirmation. Here's what we found:

H1 · Info Scatter Confirmed

Over three quarters of respondents use three or more sources when preparing for a trip, and more than half spend over two hours doing so. Both thresholds met. The pain is real, and it's about the process, not the availability of information.

H2 · Passport Inequity Inconclusive

Only 24.5% reported visa-related stress, well below our 70% threshold. But the sample told the story: over 90% held strong European passports. We simply didn't have the right respondents to test this properly. The hypothesis wasn't falsified. It just couldn't be confirmed.

H3 · Source Trust Partial

62.3% reported encountering conflicting information, meeting our threshold. But average trust came in at 3.66 out of 5, above the 3.0 ceiling we'd set. People find the information confusing, but they don't distrust it as deeply as we assumed. The pain is residual anxiety, not an outright inability to trust what they find.

H4 · Hidden Risk Partial

48.1% were unaware of insurance gaps tied to safety advisories, less than two percentage points below our 50% threshold. The caught-out rate for entry or vaccination issues sat at 28.3%, below the 40% bar. Close, but not confirmed. Again, the European-heavy sample likely depressed these numbers.

H5 · Scope Breadth Confirmed

This was the standout finding. 69.8% rated advance booking information at four or higher out of five, and 75.5% had been caught out at an attraction that required a reservation they hadn't known about. Both metrics exceeded our thresholds by more than double. Advance booking isn't a nice-to-have. It's a must-have for the MVP.

Product appetite is strong: 73.6% of respondents rated the platform concept four or higher out of five, with a mean of 4.09. The trust model is clear: official data, freshness, and traveller reviews.

The Feedback

One piece of advice that changes everything for H2.

The presentation itself went well. The cohort and our professor could see the rigour: a clean thread from theory to interviews to refined hypotheses to quantitative evidence. The JTBD framework resonated. The honest treatment of partial and inconclusive results rather than spinning them as wins was noted positively.

But the sharpest feedback came on H2, passport inequity. Our professor pointed out that the reason we couldn't confirm it wasn't a flaw in the hypothesis. It was a flaw in the instrument. Our survey sent every respondent through the same set of questions regardless of their passport strength. The result was that weak-passport holders, who made up a small fraction of our sample, were drowned out by the European majority.

"Restructure the survey into two paths. One for EU passport holders, one for non-EU. That way you can actually isolate whether the burden differs."

It sounds simple, but it changes the entire measurement design. Instead of hoping for a balanced sample and running a single analysis, we create two distinct question flows. EU holders get one set of questions calibrated to their experience. Non-EU holders get another, with additional items on visa friction, preparation time, and bureaucratic complexity. Same core constructs, but tailored to surface the differences that a one-size-fits-all survey buries.

This is exactly what the next survey round will do. We're rebuilding the instrument with this dual-path structure and actively recruiting non-EU passport holders for the next wave. If the pain gap is real, this design will find it.

What's Next

From problem validation to solution validation.

The overall verdict from our evidence is: proceed, with scope refinements. The core problem is validated. Advance booking joins the MVP. Passport inequity requires retesting with the right instrument and the right sample.

Here's what we're doing this week:

Rebuilding the survey With the dual-path structure our professor recommended. EU and non-EU flows, same research questions, different question sets.
Recruiting non-EU passport holders Through international student networks and expat communities. This is the segment our first round missed.
Moving to solution validation With prototype interviews on the combined MVP: visa requirements, health guidance, safety advisories, and advance booking information, all in one place.
Using trust factors as our design brief The survey told us what earns trust: official data sources (71.7%), information freshness (66%), and real traveller reviews (62.3%). Those three principles will guide every design decision.

Two and a half weeks ago we had an idea. A week later we had interviews. Then survey data from 53 people. Now we have a validated problem and a clear direction. The evidence isn't perfect, and we're not pretending it is. H2 still needs an answer. H3 and H4 need sharper instruments. But the foundation is solid, and we know what to build.

We'll be back next week with our solution validation results. Stay tuned.
