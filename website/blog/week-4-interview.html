What a Marketing CEO Taught Us About Building a Travel Platform People Actually Trust

We sat down with Thomas Laranjo, CEO of House of Communication UK. One conversation that sharpened everything: community, trust, and monetisation done right.

One of the most striking things about building a startup is how much you don't know, and how quickly a single conversation with the right person can sharpen your thinking. This week, we had the privilege of sitting down with Thomas Laranjo, CEO of House of Communication UK, the largest independent agency group in Europe, which specialises in creating campaigns that build brands holistically and make them successful. The conversation was frank, generous, and for a team that has spent weeks wrestling with questions of trust, channels and community, it was enormously clarifying.

The Expert

Who Is Thomas Laranjo, and Why Did We Reach Out?

Tom has spent over twenty years working across media strategy, behavioural planning and brand communications with some of the world's best-known organisations. In his current role, he leads House of Communication UK, which brings together creative, media, consulting and digital capabilities under a single integrated structure. What made him particularly relevant to us is that his work sits right at the intersection of consumer behaviour, trust, and how brands find and keep audiences — which maps almost perfectly onto the challenges Travel Lab is trying to solve.

We wanted a perspective that none of us had access to internally: someone who understands both how people make decisions under uncertainty and how young businesses build credibility from scratch.

The Connection

How We Made the Connection

The connection came through a combination of LinkedIn outreach and a personal introduction. One of our team members reached out to Tom via LinkedIn, with the help of a family contact who had studied alongside him during their undergraduate years. Tom had not previously met any of us, and our project was entirely new to him. That mix of a direct LinkedIn approach and a warm referral through an old acquaintance is a good illustration of how weak-tie networking tends to work in practice: it was not a cold message into the void, but nor was it a conversation with someone already in our circle. It was the kind of bridge-building that entrepreneurs rely on to access knowledge and perspectives that simply are not available closer to home.

The Insights

What Tom Told Us, and Why It Matters

Generational behaviour is the lens that changes everything

Tom's opening point was one we hadn't fully interrogated. He argued that search behaviours among 18 to 35-year-olds are substantially different from those of older generations, and that the depth of their information-gathering scales with the emotional and financial weight of whatever they are doing. For something like international travel, which is high-cost and high-emotion, young people are not simplifying their research. If anything, they are expanding it. They use AI tools for an initial answer, then validate through search, and then turn to their social networks to check that answer further.

This matters for us. If our target users are naturally inclined to consult multiple sources, we cannot just promise them a shortcut. We need to earn the right to be their anchor — the platform they return to because it has proven itself more reliable than the alternatives.

An information platform is not enough: community is the product

Tom was emphatic here, and it reinforced something we had already begun to sense from our own research. A platform that aggregates visa requirements, health advisories and safety information is useful, but it is not compelling enough on its own to generate loyalty or habit. What would make Travel Lab genuinely sticky, in his view, is the community layer: the opportunity for a traveller heading to Hanoi or Barcelona to connect with people who actually live there, not just to read about a destination but to begin a relationship with it before departure.

He pointed to a broader trend he is seeing across major travel platforms. Airbnb, for instance, is leaning heavily into localised, authentic experiences. Travel Lab has an opportunity to reflect that same orientation, positioning itself not as a booking tool but as the space where the emotional preparation for a journey begins.

Monetisation should follow the information, not interrupt it

One of the more practical suggestions from the conversation was around how Travel Lab might generate revenue in a way that improves the user experience rather than detracting from it. Tom's idea was straightforward: if you advise someone to get a particular vaccination before travelling to Southeast Asia, link directly to clinics where they can book it, ideally with a discount attached. If you recommend a restaurant area in a city, make it actionable. The information becomes a springboard, and each action is a potential commercial relationship.

This gives us a sharper sense of where our affiliate revenue stream could sit without feeling intrusive or out of place.

Trust has to be borrowed before it can be built

Perhaps the most immediately useful part of the conversation was Tom's thinking on how to build trust at launch, which is the very question we put to him directly. His answer drew on platforms we all know. Uber had to recruit a large pool of drivers before riders would trust it. Airbnb had to build up property listings before guests believed the platform was legitimate. For Travel Lab, Tom suggested three equivalents.

Recruit credible partners early Travel health clinics, verified local guides, or recognised travel organisations whose presence signals that the platform is serious.
Pursue independent industry accreditation He cited ABTA recognition as a meaningful trust signal within travel, but the broader principle holds: third-party verification matters enormously when you have no track record of your own.
Actively encourage early adopters to leave reviews A strong and growing Trustpilot score, accumulated quickly, can do more for perceived credibility than months of marketing spend.
"In the absence of your own brand equity, borrow it from others. Surround yourself with names and organisations that users already trust, and let that association carry the weight until you have earned the right to stand on your own."

What's Next

What This Means for Travel Lab

Tom's advice arrives at a pivotal moment for us. We have validated that the problem is real. Fragmented, distrusted travel information is genuinely making preparation harder than it needs to be, and we are now moving into solution validation with an MVP focused on visa requirements, health guidance, safety advisories and advance booking essentials.

His input sharpens our thinking in three concrete ways:

Frame Travel Lab not just as an information aggregator but as a community — a place where preparation becomes connection. Build trust before we have a track record, through targeted partnerships, third-party accreditation and early-adopter reviews. Reframe our monetisation not as a compromise but as a natural extension of the user journey.

We are genuinely grateful to Tom for his time and his willingness to engage seriously with a group of students who reached out to him cold. It is a useful reminder that some of the most valuable conversations in entrepreneurship are with people you have not yet met.
