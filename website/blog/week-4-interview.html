Two Expert Conversations: What We Learned About Trust and Community

We spoke with a marketing industry CEO and a travel content creator. Different worlds, the same core message: trust is the product before the product is the product.

This week we had two expert conversations that, taken together, gave us a much sharper picture of what Travel Lab needs to become. The first was with Thomas Laranjo, CEO of House of Communication UK, a senior voice in brand strategy and consumer behaviour. The second was with Miranda, a travel and van life content creator who has built a real online community from scratch on Instagram and TikTok. One conversation came from the top of the industry. The other came from the ground level of exactly the world our users live in. Both pointed in the same direction.

Expert Interview 1

Thomas Laranjo · CEO, House of Communication UK

Who Is Tom, and Why Did We Reach Out?

Tom has spent over twenty years working across media strategy, behavioural planning and brand communications with some of the world's best-known organisations. In his current role, he leads House of Communication UK, the largest independent agency group in Europe, which brings together creative, media, consulting and digital capabilities under a single integrated structure. What made him particularly relevant to us is that his work sits right at the intersection of consumer behaviour, trust, and how brands find and keep audiences — which maps almost perfectly onto the challenges Travel Lab is trying to solve.

How We Made the Connection

The connection came through a combination of LinkedIn outreach and a personal introduction. One of our team members reached out to Tom via LinkedIn, with the help of a family contact who had studied alongside him during their undergraduate years. Tom had not previously met any of us. That mix of a direct LinkedIn approach and a warm referral through an old acquaintance is a good illustration of how weak-tie networking tends to work in practice: it was not a cold message into the void, but nor was it a conversation with someone already in our circle.

What Tom Told Us

Generational behaviour is the lens that changes everything. Tom argued that 18 to 35-year-olds do not simplify their research for high-cost, high-emotion decisions like international travel. They use AI for an initial answer, validate through search, then check again through social networks. We cannot just promise a shortcut. We need to earn the right to be their anchor.

An information platform is not enough: community is the product. A platform that aggregates visa requirements and safety advisories is useful, but not sticky on its own. What would make Travel Lab genuinely habit-forming is the community layer: connecting a traveller heading to Hanoi with someone who actually lives there, before departure. He pointed to Airbnb's move toward localised, authentic experiences as a parallel.

Monetisation should follow the information, not interrupt it. If we advise someone to get a vaccination before Southeast Asia, link directly to clinics where they can book it. If we recommend a neighbourhood in a city, make it actionable. Each piece of advice becomes a potential commercial relationship without feeling intrusive.

Trust has to be borrowed before it can be built. Uber needed drivers before riders trusted it. Airbnb needed listings before guests believed in it. Tom's advice for Travel Lab: recruit credible partners early, pursue independent accreditation such as ABTA recognition, and encourage early adopters to leave reviews. A growing Trustpilot score can outperform months of marketing spend.

"In the absence of your own brand equity, borrow it from others. Surround yourself with names and organisations that users already trust, and let that association carry the weight until you have earned the right to stand on your own."

Expert Interview 2

Miranda · Travel and Van Life Content Creator

Who Is Miranda, and Why Did We Reach Out?

Miranda is a full-time content creator who documents her travels and van life on Instagram and TikTok. What started as sharing her personal life has grown into a genuine community of travelers and van lifers who follow her for inspiration, tips, and an honest look at life on the road. We reached out because Travel Lab is not just an information platform. We want to build a community layer on top of it, and Miranda has done exactly that from scratch. Her experience felt directly relevant to the challenge in front of us.

How We Made the Connection

We found Miranda through Instagram and reached out directly through the platform. She had no prior connection to any of our team before this conversation, which makes her feedback particularly valuable as a fully independent perspective. It is also a useful reminder that for a platform built around travel, the creator community is a natural network to tap into for grounded, honest feedback.

What Miranda Told Us

The information scatter problem is real, and creators feel it too. Miranda recognised our core problem immediately from her own experience as a creator. Travel content is fragmented by design: one creator covers a beach, another a restaurant, another a visa tip. There is no single place where someone planning a trip to a specific country can find everything. Having it all in one place, she said, would be "pretty handy."

People will switch, but only if they trust you and know you exist. Her answer was honest and conditional. Yes, she thinks people would move to a single platform, but two things need to be true: the platform needs to be known, and it needs to be trusted. The information has to be factual and recent. Outdated content kills credibility fast.

Building a community requires consistency and a human face. Miranda grew her audience by showing up consistently and letting people see who she was. She drew a direct parallel for Travel Lab: people want to know who is behind what they are reading.

"When I go to a website to buy something or get information, I always go to the About Us page. I want to know who built it and why."

That single observation had an immediate effect on how we are thinking about the platform. An About section is not just a nice-to-have. For first-time visitors with no prior reason to trust us, it is a trust signal in its own right.

What This Means for Travel Lab

Two Perspectives, One Direction

What is striking about these two conversations is how much they converge despite coming from completely different vantage points. Tom approached trust from a brand strategy perspective. Miranda approached it from lived experience as a creator. Both arrived at the same conclusion: the platform has to feel human before it can feel authoritative.

Make our team visible An About section that shows the people behind Travel Lab, the research we have done, and why we built it. Miranda checks this page on every website she visits. It is a trust signal, not a formality.
Borrow credibility through partnerships Tom's advice: recruit travel health clinics, verified guides, or recognised organisations whose presence signals the platform is serious. Third-party association matters most when you have no track record yet.
Keep information fresh and sourced Both experts, independently, emphasised that outdated or unverifiable information is the fastest way to lose an audience. Every data point on Travel Lab carries a verified date for exactly this reason.
Build community from the start, not as an afterthought Tom sees community as the stickiness layer that turns information into loyalty. Miranda built hers through consistency and authenticity. The lesson for us is to start that work now, not after the product is finished.
Let monetisation serve the user journey Tom's framing is the one we are adopting: every piece of advice is a springboard. Link the vaccination tip to the clinic. Link the transport tip to the booking. Revenue that helps the user is revenue that does not feel like revenue.
Two experts. Two completely different professional worlds. The same core message: people trust people before they trust platforms. Build the human layer first, and the rest follows.

We are grateful to both Tom and Miranda for their time and their candour. Neither had any obligation to engage with a group of students, and both gave us conversations that genuinely moved our thinking forward. That, in itself, is a small lesson in how to build the kind of community we are trying to create.
